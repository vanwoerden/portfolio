Decreasing the Distance to Data

By putting live data in text at the top of the page, we matched our work on COVID-19 vaccinations to the readers' expectations.

live example: ourworldindata.org/covid-vaccinations
Users: anyone interested in regular updates on the COVID-19 vaccinations rollout
When: June 2021
Team: founder, writer/researchers, data scientists, engineer
My role: design, evangelism, product management, reporting
Outcome: great success

Background

Our entries were getting long. We had a lot of elements we thought were important, and moving them to the top and emphasizing them were the main two ways to reflect that importance. But if everything's important, nothing is. We saw user feedback coming in that our entries were long and that it was hard to find a quick overview of the most important things. On desktop, no data was visible on the first screen. On mobile, the distance to data was more than 2 screens! For a website called our world in data, that's not great.

Too many things at the top of the page competing for the user's attention. None of these things were actual data

Google is more convenient for getting a quick update

We actually lost this user to Google, because he just wanted a quick update, and Google did a better job at that. The Google search results page for COVID-19 vaccinations puts data front and center. There's some space reserved for navigation, and a fraction of the screen is taken up by metadata.

“That's where I started [at Our World in Data], you know a year ago, and then I was seeing a lot of very detailed information, and mostly what I was interested in was just tracking the number of detected cases and fatalities for certain regions over time.

Google has a very, you know you just get there very easily. That gives me that first order of information if you will.”

Don't make me think

This is Steve Krug's classic almost to a tee. This user really felt we were overdoing it with the details.

We have navigation (arguably necessary), metadata, our mission, a link to our dataset, but no actual data or research on a 1920x1080px screen resolution.

“I noticed you also did something like this for covid, and then I got used to it and it was ok, but looking at this for the first time, you're just making me work too much and it's too many words.”

“I think right now I trust you guys, that you did that [vetting data and sources] so I don't need to know much about it. So I'm looking forward to more insights and less text. Make me work less, for what I'm learning.”

Does this belong here?

We put too many elements at the top of the page that don't serve a primary purpose. If everything's important, nothing is.

About the 'our work belongs to everyone' block:

“There are times when I'm like oh my god this is great, I wanna play around with this all day, and there are other times where I'm like, I just want to read this quickly.”

But don't remove all of these details

Academic researchers are much more used to reading detailed texts; but even for them, it's hard to imagine that having a quick overview (or abstract, if you will) of what's to come wouldn't be helpful.

“The amount of information, it was thorough, but not too long, not too complicated, it gave a good overview without being too detailed.”

“Then when I read the text and there is reference to the charts I will start looking at the charts.”

“I will usually use some time to maneuver a little around the pages when I look at them, and then I usually do find what I want. Maybe not in the first try, but relatively easy.”

Live data in text

We were dreaming about being able to use live data in our writing, but that was technically challenging to get right. Consider the following sentence: In 2000, the UK emitted 2.45 tons of coal per capita.

When the underlying data changes, we could automatically update this to:

In 2010, the UK emitted 1.86 tons of coal per capita.

Now imagine we add some context, for example: In 2000, the UK emitted 2.45 tons of coal per capita—0.59 tons more than China.

Our automatic update becomes tricky when China's emissions surpass those of the UK in 2004; a phrase like that's very close to China makes more sense for 2003, and after that it should be less than China.

And since (global) progress is all about changes over time, not being able to add this contextual layer to the writing would in many cases only be a partial solution for our authors. Big problems require a long-term view, looking at changes over years, decades, sometimes centuries, and organizations publishing data on these problems often do so only once a year.

COVID-19 data: a different beast

From December 1 to July 31, 2021, 64% of people that visited our page on COVID-19 vaccinations were returning users. In the same period, the percentage of returning users was 53% sitewide. Pre-pandemic—in 2019—that number sat at 29%.

A much more active audience and a dataset that was updated daily made it much more appealing to go ahead with a form of live data in text. It also became much more important to start with the data users were coming to this and other covid-related pages for.

Design

Live data at the top, the data explorer (at least partially) in view on desktop. In the end, having the authors' names prominently at the top was—surprisingly—less of a requirement than I had thought, and we just moved the names to the bottom of the page.

final design

Outcomes

Changes we hoped to see were: an increase in return visits to the vaccinations page, more interaction with the data explorer since it was now closer to the top of the page, no negative user feedback about moving the 'our work belongs to everyone block', and no significant decrease in dataset downloads.

Changes we actually saw:

  • No significant increase in return visits or interaction with the data explorer
  • a decrease in clicks on links to the dataset, which we mitigated by linking to it elsewhere. And also, the fact that it used to be the first link perhaps played a role in its popularity
  • no user complaints
  • A decrease in the ratio of scroll events divided by page views for the vaccinations page of 0.74 to 0.59

Underappreciated in dataviz: Well-crafted text, highlighting the key numbers. High information density, high accessibility, often the best intro to a complex dashboard.

Broader context of decreasing distance to data

Ultimately, the goal behind these changes is to get data closer to everyone who's interested in it and especially to people who can use it to make decisions that affect real long-term progress. These are only two examples of how we aim to accomplish this goal. My role is often to ask why a few times when new ideas come up amongst the founders, so we go from "let's build a super powerful and flexible data analysis tool" to the goal behind that and then suggesting that perhaps a more efficient way to reach that goal is to make our data or visualization tools more easily available inside of existing tools and platforms out there like Jupyter notebooks or Observable, or having our data show up directly on Google's SERPs.
