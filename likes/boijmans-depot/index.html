There are good reasons for this. Gallery space is limited. Light, temperature, and humidity all affect the lifespan of an artwork; optimal viewing and optimal conserving conditions are not the same.

Historically, many museums started out eargerly growing their collection. Not much attention was paid to storage. Nor were most museums very discriminate about what art to accept and what to deny:

As the number of artworks grew, so did the problem of conservation. Over time, the role of the museum changed from collector and exposer to conservator and keeper. But where is the balance between showing what you have vs preserving your collection for future generations?

Culturally, it's very much not done to cull collections. The name for it is telling: deaccessioning. Literally, the process of removing access. Yes, let's come up with a word to hide in plain sight what we want to do but we think is kind of not acceptable: GETTING RID OF PRISTINE PRECIOUS PUBLIC GOODS. Once you have the art, you're stuck with it. In the US, there are laws that prohibit (or lawsuits that are filed) museums from selling anything in their collection.
